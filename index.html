Workshop Aims and Scope

Ethical considerations have received growing attention in industrial and academic spheres, as AI-based systems increasingly influence every facet of our lives â€“ including who receives a job, who is subjected to increased policing, and whose livelihoods are automated away. It is now commonplace to see news stories reporting harmful impacts of existing AI systems deployed in various real world, high-stakes contexts. Pattern analysis and machine intelligence applications that leverage computer vision are not immune to the risks observed in other domains, and recent years have raised attention to potential pitfalls of applications empowered with computer vision. Yet despite this widespread attention, issues of fairness, accountability, transparency, and ethics are still under-explored in the computer vision community, and recent work has emphasized that design requirements and methods for addressing challenges related to these issues are highly domain-dependent. Thus, to complement domain-general investigations common in the broader research literature, it becomes critical that our computer vision research community explores what fairness, accountability, transparency, and ethics mean in computer vision applications specifically. Such a critical aspect is the key for enhancing our understanding of these aspects on computer vision applications more broadly, building on knowledge gleaned from years of computer vision technology research.

Workshop Topics

The workshop welcomes contributions in all topics related to fairness, accountability, transparency, and ethics in pattern recognition and machine intelligence applications, with special attention to computer vision, focused (but not limited) to:

  • Modelling:
    • Fairness of pattern recognition and classification models (e.g., low confidence predictions, disbalanced data)
    • Accountability of pattern recognition models (e.g., accountability for different stakeholders, requirements on modeling to enable accountability)
    • Transparency of pattern recognition models (e.g., explanatory needs for different user groups, explaining individual and global prediction patterns)
    • Privacy and security in pattern recognition models (e.g., privacy-preserving models, robustness to attacks, protection of model user's representation)
  • Countermeasures:
    • Fairness of pattern recognition (e.g., trade-offs between criteria, bias for classes of users)
    • Accountability of pattern recognition (e.g., mechanisms for reporting or accounting, balancing data and completeness)
    • Transparency of pattern recognition (e.g., explanatory visualizations and users' interfaces, taxonomies for explanations, comparing explanatory aims)
    • Privacy and security of pattern recognition (e.g., user control of shared sensitive attributes, multi-task learning for trade-offs between privacy and accuracy)
  • Assessment:
    • Methodologies to assess fairness (e.g., metrics for balance, diversity, and other social welfare criteria; evaluation simulations; assessing stakeholder specific bias)
    • Methodologies to assess accountability (e.g., metrics and user studies of accountability mechanisms, studies to assess accountability of existing systems)
    • Methodologies to assess transparency (e.g., metrics, protocols, and evaluation frameworks for assessing the impact of interface or interaction strategies)
    • Methodologies to assess privacy and security (e.g., metrics, protocols, and evaluation frameworks for assessing privacy and robustness)
  • Impacts:
    • Impacts of fairness practices (e.g., balancing needs of different stakeholders)
    • Impacts of accountability practices (e.g., mechanisms for reporting decisions about models)
    • Impacts of transparency practices (e.g., counterfactuals and what-if predictions)
    • Impacts of privacy practices (e.g., user studies to measure strategies for privacy control)
  • Applications:
    • Action and behavior recognition
    • Biometric recognition
    • Computational photography
    • Image and video retrieval
    • Medical, biological, and cell microscopy
    • Scene analysis and understanding
    • Vision for robotics and autonomous vehicles
    • ... and more related

Submission Details

We invite authors to submit 8-page unpublished original papers, with additional pages containing only cited references allowed.

All submissions will go through a double-blind review process by at least three reviewers and must be formatted according to the Latex template of ICCV 2021. Authors should consult the ICCV paper guidelines for the preparation of their papers. All contributions must be submitted as PDF files to https://easychair.org/conferences/?conf=RPRMI2021.

Accepted papers will be published in the official ICCV Workshops proceedings and archived in IEEE Xplore Digital Library and the Computer Vision Foundation (CVF) Open Access archive.

We expect authors, PC, and the organizing committee to adhere to the ICCV 2021's policies.
